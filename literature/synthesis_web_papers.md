### Paper 1 — Aryal, Ghimire, Tiwari, Baaniya & Pandey (2024)
**"Evolution and Future Prospects of Hydropower Sector in Nepal: A Review"**
*Heliyon* | DOI: 10.1016/j.heliyon.2024.e31139 | Open Access (PMC)

**What they did:** Systematic review of 22 peer-reviewed articles + government datasets (NEA, DOED, WECS). Spatial mapping of 108 hydropower projects in QGIS. Demand forecasting using NEA historical data (2006/07–2020/21).

**Core quantitative findings:**
| Indicator | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Theoretical hydropower potential | 83 GW |
| Technically achievable potential | 43 GW (2019 estimate) |
| Installed capacity (2021) | 2,190 MW |
| Hydropower share of total national energy | **4%** (2019) — electricity ≠ total energy |
| Biomass share of total energy | **72%** (2019) |
| Daily load shedding at worst | 18 hours (dry season peak, 2012–2013) |
| Demand growth equation | y = 386.78x + 2772.7 (R² = 0.9911) — near-linear |
| 2030 demand projections | 20,074–29,864 GWh (BAU to high scenario) |
| 2040 demand projection | 115,294 GWh (high scenario) |
| Cross-border export capacity | 365 MW to India (2022–2023) |

**Key insight missed by most headlines:** Hydropower supplies only **4% of Nepal's total energy** — the rest is biomass cooking fuel. The electricity transition is real but narrow. Policy that focuses only on the electricity sector addresses a small fraction of Nepal's actual energy poverty.

**Storage vs. run-of-river:** Run-of-river plants operate at 40–50% capacity in dry seasons; storage-type plants maintain 88%. Yet investor reluctance (high capital cost, longer construction) has kept Nepal overwhelmingly run-of-river — creating the monsoon surplus / dry-season deficit problem.

**Institutional obstacles (multi-layered):**
- Social: community displacement, water rights disputes
- Political: governmental instability (29 governments since 1990), inter-ministry coordination failures
- Economic: NEA's weak financial health, tariff-cost misalignment
- Technical: poor hydrological monitoring, limited transmission
- Environmental: GLOF risks, landslide susceptibility

**Government targets (ambitious, likely missed again):**
- 2026: 10,000 MW capacity; dry-season exporter status
- 2030: 15% clean energy in total mix; 90% electric vehicle penetration
- 2045: Carbon neutrality

**Reliability assessment:** HIGH. Peer-reviewed, open access, primary government data, QGIS mapping.

### Paper 2 — Poudel & Kumar (2025)
**"Review of Energy Policies and Strategies in Nepal: Challenges and Opportunities"**
*International Journal of Sustainable and Green Energy*, Vol. 14(2), pp. 66–79 | DOI: 10.11648/j.ijsge.20251402.11

**What they did:** PESTLE analysis (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental) + SWOT framework + global comparative benchmarking of Nepal against international energy transition patterns.

**Framework findings:**

| SWOT Element | Key Points |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Strengths** | Abundant hydro resources; government commitment to carbon neutrality; growing installed capacity |
| **Weaknesses** | Seasonal variability; regulatory inefficiency; inadequate grid infrastructure; limited financial capacity |
| **Opportunities** | Regional energy trade (India, Bangladesh, potentially Pakistan); growing clean energy investment globally |
| **Threats** | Climate change impacts on runoff; investment uncertainty; fossil fuel price volatility affecting imports |

**Policy diagnosis:** The binding constraint is not resource availability — Nepal has 43 GW of viable potential — but **regulatory inefficiency and weak PPP frameworks**. Laws exist but implementation lags. The private sector (BOT model) has driven most recent capacity additions, but is constrained by tariff uncertainty and grid connection delays.

**Global positioning:** Nepal is classified as early-stage in the energy transition curve — similar to Sub-Saharan Africa in electrification trajectory, but with a uniquely favorable resource base.

**Reliability assessment:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Published journal; analytical framework solid; limited quantitative evidence (no econometric analysis). Useful for policy framing, not causal inference.

### Paper 3 — Wang & Khan (2024)
**"Renewable Energy and CO₂ Emissions in Developing and Developed Nations: A Panel Estimate Approach"**
*Frontiers in Environmental Science* | DOI: 10.3389/fenvs.2024.1405001

**What they did:** Panel data analysis across 35 developing + 20 developed nations, 1970–2022. Methods: Generalized Linear Model (GLM) + Robust Least Squares (RLS). Examined relationships between renewable energy adoption, GDP growth, energy intensity, CO₂ emissions intensity.

**Core findings:**
1. **Threshold effect (counterintuitive):** When developing nations increase renewable energy beyond their *absorption capacity*, per-capita CO₂ emissions can **rise** — not fall. Explanation: rapid renewable scaling requires grid upgrades that often run on diesel/fossil backup; intermittency backed by gas peakers; construction emissions.
2. **Energy dependence accelerates all components** in both country groups — import dependence increases energy intensity, emissions intensity, and crowds out renewable development.
3. **Regional spillover effects:** Neighboring countries experience correlated energy dependency impacts — energy policy has cross-border externalities, especially in South Asia.
4. **Institutional quality moderates outcomes:** Wealthy nations with stronger institutions showed the strongest renewable adoption effects — consistent with Shittu et al. (2024).

**Policy implication:** Developing countries should not simply maximize renewable capacity additions; they must build absorptive capacity (grid infrastructure, storage, skilled workforce) in parallel. A "build it and the emissions will fall" assumption is empirically wrong for developing economies.

**Critical evaluation:** The GLM/RLS methodology is relatively standard; the threshold effect is the novel finding but is not identified causally (endogeneity not addressed). Results should be treated as correlational, not causal. The 1970 start date includes era of very different energy systems — pre-and-post period pooling may obscure structural changes.

**Relevance to our proposals:** Directly motivates the cost-of-capital specification (financial market development needed alongside renewable policy) and the AEPC evaluation (subsidy effectiveness depends on absorptive capacity of target districts).

**Reliability assessment:** MEDIUM. Published in peer-reviewed journal; methodology straightforward but identification weak; findings directionally informative.

### Paper 4 — Shittu, Saqib, Abdul Latiff & Baharudin (2024)
**"Energy Subsidies and Energy Access in Developing Countries: Does Institutional Quality Matter?"**
*SAGE Open* | DOI: 10.1177/21582440241271118

*(Full text returned 403 — summarized from search result and abstract)*

**What they did:** Panel regression across developing countries; institutional quality (ICRG indices) as interaction term with energy subsidy variables. Outcome: energy access rates.

**Core finding:** Energy subsidies improve access most where institutions are **strong enough to enforce distribution and prevent rent capture**. In weak-institution contexts, subsidies are captured by elites or inefficiently allocated — the energy access impact is near-zero or negative.

**Mechanism:** Institutional quality moderates the subsidy → access link through two channels: (1) enforcement — whether subsidy reaches intended beneficiaries; (2) targeting — whether allocation is need-based or captured by politically connected groups.

**Relevance:** This is the theoretical backbone for Proposal 3 (AEPC evaluation). The cross-country finding needs within-country subnational testing — which is exactly what the Nepal district-level DID would provide.

**Reliability assessment:** MEDIUM-HIGH (from search summary; full text inaccessible — verify before citing in proposals).

### Paper 5 — Anonymous (2024) [Nature Energy]
**"Reducing the Cost of Capital to Finance the Energy Transition in Developing Countries"**
*Nature Energy* | DOI: 10.1038/s41560-024-01606-7

*(Paywalled — summarized from search result and abstract)*

**What they did:** Theoretical model + simulation of how financing cost differentials between developed and developing countries affect the pace and cost of the energy transition.

**Core findings:**
- Alleviating financial constraints benefits **both climate and equity outcomes simultaneously** — a rare positive-sum result
- The cost-of-capital gap (roughly 8–10 percentage points between rich and poor countries) explains a substantial share of the renewable deployment gap
- Concessional lending and risk guarantees are the most effective policy instruments — more effective than domestic policy reform alone in the short run

**Gap this paper leaves:** The theoretical mechanism is modeled and simulated but never empirically tested at scale across a large panel of developing countries. This is precisely the gap our research specification fills.

**Reliability assessment:** HIGH for theoretical framing (Nature Energy standard); empirical grounding limited to simulation — explicitly noted as a gap.

## Cross-Paper Synthesis

### Theme 1: Nepal's Energy Transition Is Narrower Than It Appears

Papers 1 and 2 together reveal a critical framing issue: **hydropower supplies only 4% of Nepal's total energy** (Paper 1, Aryal et al. 2024). Biomass — wood, dung, agricultural residue — accounts for 72%. When international observers celebrate Nepal's "renewable energy success," they are describing a narrow electricity sector transformation that has barely touched the cooking energy system used by most Nepali households, particularly in rural areas.

This has two implications for research design:
1. Studies using electricity consumption as a proxy for energy use systematically understate Nepal's energy poverty
2. The energy–growth nexus (Proposal 1) should be estimated separately for electricity (grid-linked, surplus) and total energy (biomass-dominated, deficit)

### Theme 2: The Scarcity-to-Surplus Transition Is Real but Fragile

Papers 1, 6, and 7 converge on a consistent picture: Nepal transitioned from deficit to surplus around 2018–2019, surplus will peak around 2025–2028 (~25 TWh), and domestic demand will absorb the surplus by the early 2030s. This creates a narrow 5–7 year window for high-value surplus allocation decisions (export, hydrogen, storage investment). All three proposals involving Nepal's surplus (Proposals 2, 5, and partially 1) are therefore **time-sensitive** — the research is most policy-relevant if completed before the window closes.

### Theme 3: Institutional Quality Is the Dominant Moderator Across All Scales

Papers 2, 3, and 4 independently arrive at the same finding: the effectiveness of energy policy and investment depends critically on institutional quality — at the country level (Papers 3 and 4), at the subnational level (Paper 4's implications), and even within Nepal's own sector governance (Paper 2). The mechanism differs slightly across papers:
- Paper 2 (Poudel & Kumar): institutions determine whether policies are implemented
- Paper 3 (Wang & Khan): institutions determine whether renewable investments translate to emissions reductions
- Paper 4 (Shittu et al.): institutions determine whether subsidies reach intended beneficiaries

For Proposal 3 (AEPC evaluation), this convergence strongly motivates the governance heterogeneity analysis — it's not just a "nice to have" robustness check, it's the theoretically central question.

### Theme 4: The Cost-of-Capital Gap Explains the Global Transition Paradox

Papers 5 and 8 together frame the global puzzle: IRENA data (Paper 8) shows renewables are now unambiguously cheaper than fossil fuels in LCOE terms in most contexts. Yet developing-country deployment lags dramatically. Paper 5 (Nature Energy) provides the theoretical resolution: LCOE comparisons are meaningless when applied at different discount rates. The 8–10 percentage-point cost-of-capital gap between rich and poor countries turns a cheaper renewable into a more expensive one.

Paper 3 (Wang & Khan) adds a complementary mechanism: even when renewables are deployed in developing countries, they may not reduce emissions in the short run if absorptive capacity (grid, backup, workforce) is inadequate. This implies the sequencing matters: financial market development → renewable deployment → grid investment → emissions reduction. Policy that skips the middle steps produces the threshold effect Wang & Khan document.

**Bottom line:** None of the Nepal-specific papers provides credible causal identification. This is the gap. The global literature (Nature Energy, Shittu et al.) has stronger methods but uses cross-country data that cannot speak to Nepal specifically. Our proposals either (a) apply quasi-experimental methods to Nepal for the first time (Proposals 1 and 3) or (b) construct new quantitative tools (Proposals 2, 4, 5) that the literature is missing.
