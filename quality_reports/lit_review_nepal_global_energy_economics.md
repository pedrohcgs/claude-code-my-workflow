# Literature Review: Nepal & Global Energy Economics

**Date:** 2026-03-08
**Query:** Nepal hydropower energy economics, global energy transition, comparative analysis across energy sectors, renewable energy policy, climate scenario modeling SSP 2023–2026

---

## Summary

The 2023–2026 frontier literature on energy economics spans three interconnected threads. First, Nepal's hydropower sector has reached an inflection point: a decade of load shedding has given way to structural electricity surplus, creating new questions about optimal allocation—cross-border export, green hydrogen production, or domestic electrification—and reopening classic debates about the hydropower–growth nexus. Second, the global energy transition has accelerated far beyond most 2020 projections: renewable LCOE has hit new floors (solar at $0.043/kWh globally in 2024), investment topped $2.3 trillion in 2025, and for the first time low-carbon sources exceeded 40% of global electricity. Third, the Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSP) framework remains the standard for climate-energy scenario work, with an ongoing update cycle (revised projections released 2025, new IAM quantifications expected 2026) that creates fresh opportunities for applied energy economics research.

Across these threads, consistent gaps emerge: (1) Nepal-specific econometric work on the energy–growth nexus using post-2018 data (the surplus era) is nearly absent in top journals; (2) cross-sector LCOE comparisons for South Asia systematically underweight hydropower's seasonal variability and storage costs; (3) renewable energy policy evaluations in developing countries rarely account for institutional quality as a heterogeneous moderator; and (4) SSP-aligned energy models for small landlocked developing economies like Nepal remain rare.

## Key Papers

### Ghimire, Tiwari et al. (2024) — "Evolution and Future Prospects of the Hydropower Sector in Nepal: A Review"
- **Journal:** *Heliyon* (ScienceDirect / PubMed Central)
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.heliyon.2024.e31139
- **Main contribution:** Comprehensive review of Nepal's hydropower development trajectory, target failures, and future demand projections through 2040
- **Method:** Narrative and quantitative review; demand forecasting
- **Key findings:**
  - Energy demand projected at 41,265 GWh (2030) and 115,294 GWh (2040)
  - Government 2018 white paper targeted 5,000 MW and 700 kWh/capita by 2023; actual outcome: 2,684 MW and 351 kWh/capita — ~50% shortfall
  - Recommends shift to storage-type plants and energy banking to manage seasonal mismatch
- **Relevance:** Essential baseline for any Nepal energy proposal; documents the institutional gap between stated targets and delivery

### Poudel & Kumar (2025) — "Review of Energy Policies and Strategies in Nepal: Challenges and Opportunities"
- **Journal:** *International Journal of Sustainable and Green Energy*, Vol. 14(2), pp. 66–79
- **DOI:** 10.11648/j.ijsge.20251402.11
- **Main contribution:** SWOT analysis of Nepal's energy policy landscape benchmarked against global low-carbon transition
- **Method:** Comparative policy analysis; SWOT framework; global policy benchmarking
- **Key findings:**
  - Strengths: abundant hydro; Weaknesses: seasonal variability, grid infrastructure gaps; Opportunities: regional trade (India, Bangladesh); Threats: climate change impacts on runoff
  - Regulatory inefficiencies and weak PPP frameworks are the binding constraints, not resource availability
- **Relevance:** Identifies policy levers most tractable for research; frames Nepal's transition positioning

### Anonymous (2024) — "Hydrogen Production from Surplus Hydropower: Techno-Economic Assessment with Alkaline Electrolysis in Nepal's Perspective"
- **Journal:** *International Journal of Hydrogen Energy*
- **Published:** June 2024
- **Main contribution:** First techno-economic assessment of Nepal as a potential green hydrogen producer using future hydropower surplus
- **Method:** Techno-economic modeling; sensitivity analysis on electricity price and electrolyzer sizing
- **Key findings:**
  - Nepal faces power shortage through 2024 but surplus begins ~2025; peaks ~25 TWh in 2028
  - LCOH highly sensitive to electricity price assumptions — small price increases substantially erode competitiveness
  - Surplus drops to ~18 TWh by 2030 as domestic demand rises
- **Relevance:** Raises the policy question of highest-value use of surplus electricity — a key proposal seed
- **⚠ Verify:** Full author list not confirmed from search; verify before citing

### Anonymous (2026) — "Socio-Economic Trade-offs of Large Hydropower Development for Community Livelihoods in Bagmati Province, Nepal"
- **Journal:** *International Journal of Energy Economics and Policy*
- **Data coverage:** 2015–2024
- **Main contribution:** Identifies development–displacement paradox in large hydro projects
- **Method:** Descriptive analysis, correlation, multiple regression, factor analysis on secondary time-series data
- **Key findings:**
  - Infrastructure and income improved alongside large hydro development
  - Environmental displacement is the strongest predictor of livelihood disruption — income gains alone insufficient to offset
- **Relevance:** Grounds the social cost of Nepal's hydro expansion; relevant for any welfare analysis or distributional proposal

### IRENA (2025) — "Renewable Power Generation Costs in 2024"
- **Publisher:** International Renewable Energy Agency
- **Published:** July 2025
- **Main contribution:** Definitive global LCOE benchmark dataset for 2024, covering all major renewable technologies
- **Key findings (2024 LCOE by technology):**
  - Onshore wind: $0.034/kWh (cheapest renewable)
  - Solar PV: $0.043/kWh
  - Hydropower: $0.057/kWh
  - 91% of new renewable capacity cheaper than cheapest new fossil fuel alternative
  - Solar PV 41% cheaper than lowest-cost fossil; onshore wind 53% cheaper
  - Battery storage fell 93% since 2010 → $192/kWh in 2024
- **Relevance:** Provides the cross-sector cost comparison baseline; essential for any LCOE or energy transition proposal

## Thematic Organization

### Theoretical Contributions

The dominant theoretical framework for Nepal energy work is the **energy–growth nexus** (Kraft & Kraft 1978 → Apergis & Payne 2009 panel extensions), which posits bi-directional or uni-directional Granger-causal relationships between energy consumption and GDP. For Nepal specifically, the nexus literature remains thin and largely pre-2018, meaning the surplus era (post load-shedding resolution) has not been studied. Globally, the theoretical frontier has shifted toward **cost-of-capital barriers** (Nature Energy 2024) and **institutional heterogeneity** as first-order determinants of transition speed in developing countries.

### Empirical Findings

**Nepal-specific:**
- Actual hydro capacity ~2,684 MW vs. 5,000 MW target (2023); per capita consumption 351 vs. 700 kWh/year target
- Electricity export surplus emerging from 2025; export revenues: NPR 17 billion (~$130M) in 2023
- ADB projects GDP 87% higher by 2030 vs. BAU if Nepal develops 1/5 of viable hydro potential
- Social costs of large hydro: environmental displacement dominates livelihood disruption (Bagmati study)

**Global energy transition:**
- 585 GW new renewable capacity added globally in 2024 (>90% of all new power)
- Low-carbon sources (renewables + nuclear) exceeded 40% of global electricity in 2024 for first time since 1940s
- Solar LCOE: $0.043/kWh; onshore wind: $0.034/kWh; hydro: $0.057/kWh (IRENA 2024)
- Battery storage LCOE fell 93% since 2010 → $192/kWh utility-scale in 2024
- Total energy transition investment: $2.3T in 2025; solar alone $554B in 2024

**Renewable policy in developing countries:**
- Institutional quality moderates subsidy → energy access link (Shittu et al. 2024)
- Drivers of renewable adoption differ between developed/developing economies; income, trade, and governance matter differently

### Methodological Innovations

- **Panel data with institutional heterogeneity:** Interaction of policy variables with institutional quality indices (ICRG, WGI) — see Shittu et al. (2024)
- **Techno-economic modeling for small-country surplus:** Nepal hydrogen studies demonstrate a project-level techno-economic approach applicable to other small-hydro exporters
- **REP/next-gen scenarios:** Post-SSP scenario frameworks being developed for AR7 cycle — methodology will need updating in proposals using long-horizon projections
- **Synthetic control and DID for policy evaluation:** Preferred for country-level renewable policy evaluation where panel variation is limited

### Open Debates

1. **Optimal use of Nepal's electricity surplus:** Export to India/Bangladesh vs. domestic industrialization vs. green hydrogen — no consensus, no published cost-benefit analysis post-2024
2. **Hydro as "green":** Nepal's run-of-river hydro has low emissions, but large storage hydro has significant ecosystem and social costs — literature has not resolved the welfare trade-off for Nepal
3. **Energy–growth causality direction for LDCs in surplus:** Classic nexus studies assumed scarcity; how do findings change when the binding constraint shifts from supply to transmission/demand?
4. **SSP applicability to small landlocked economies:** SSPs are calibrated for large country groups; Nepal's remittance-driven, India-dependent energy system fits poorly into standard SSP narratives

## Gaps and Opportunities

1. **Nepal energy–growth nexus: post-surplus era (2018–present).** No journal paper uses post-load-shedding data to re-examine the Granger-causality direction. A panel/time-series study using NEA and WDI data through 2024 would fill a genuine gap. Short-run vs. long-run dynamics likely differ now that supply is no longer binding.

2. **Cross-sector LCOE comparison for South Asia.** Global IRENA benchmarks do not disaggregate for South Asian hydropower's seasonal cost profile. A study comparing effective LCOE of Nepalese run-of-river hydro (accounting for seasonal mismatch, transmission loss, and export treaty terms) against solar+storage alternatives would be novel and policy-relevant.

3. **Renewable energy policy effectiveness in Nepal: the institutional channel.** AEPC subsidies and NEA tariff reform have not been rigorously evaluated using difference-in-differences or synthetic control. The Shittu et al. (2024) framework offers a template; Nepal's subnational variation (development regions, rural electrification rollout timing) provides identification variation.

4. **SSP-aligned energy demand scenarios for Nepal.** Country-level IAM application of SSPs to Nepal is absent in published literature. A proposal that downscales SSP2/SSP3 energy demand projections with Nepal-specific parameters (remittance growth, appliance ownership, urbanization) would generate a useful planning tool.

5. **Green hydrogen feasibility under realistic trade assumptions.** The two hydrogen studies assume full electricity price pass-through from NEA. If Nepal's cross-border PTA pricing constrains effective export revenue, the cost-of-hydrogen calculus changes substantially. No study incorporates the PTA pricing regime explicitly.
